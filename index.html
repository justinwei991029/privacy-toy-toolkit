The Privacy Issue: AI Smart Toys & the Internet of Toys

The specific privacy issue this toolkit focuses on is how AI-powered smart toys collect, store, and use children‚Äôs data. These toys are part of the ‚ÄúInternet of Toys‚Äù (IoToys), where devices in children‚Äôs bedrooms and playrooms are connected to cloud services and machine learning systems.

Privacy Issue & Context

What Makes Smart Toys Different?

Always-on microphones Cloud-based AI Emotional bonding

Smart toys go beyond traditional dolls or action figures. Many can listen to children‚Äôs voices, respond with personalized conversations, and even adapt over time based on a child‚Äôs emotions, interests, and behaviour.

  • They often upload recordings and interactions to company servers.
  • They can infer mood, preferences, and routines.
  • Data may be kept long-term and used for profiling.

Research on smart connected toys shows that their design encourages children to trust them, making it more likely that they share intimate details about their lives and families.

Why This Issue Is Compelling

Scholars, policy organizations, and journalists have all raised concerns:

  • Researchers argue that smart toys are ‚Äúaffectively sticky,‚Äù meaning children bond with them emotionally and may overshare sensitive information.
  • Policy reports show that children say they want more control over how their data is collected and used, but current designs rarely give them meaningful choices.
  • Media coverage highlights cases where companies stored children‚Äôs voice recordings indefinitely or where toys were vulnerable to hacking.

Together, these sources point to the same problem: innovation in AI toys is moving quickly, but children‚Äôs privacy protections, literacy, and rights-based governance are lagging behind.

Understanding the data flow is a key step in privacy literacy. Below is a simplified path that many AI toys follow, from a child‚Äôs voice to corporate servers and potential third parties.

From Bedroom to Data Centre: A Simple Map

Step 1

The toy‚Äôs microphone picks up the child‚Äôs voice, and sometimes background conversations as well. Wake words may not be as clear or limited as advertised.

Step 2

The toy sends audio recordings over the internet to the company‚Äôs servers. These recordings can be stored, sometimes indefinitely, unless deleted.

Step 3

The toy replies in a friendly way and may adapt its stories, games, and prompts to keep the child engaged, based on the profile.

Many of these steps are described in privacy policies, but often in vague or technical language. A key goal of this toolkit is to translate those policies into clear, actionable information for families.

Children‚Äôs Rights in the Digital World

Children are not just users or consumers; they are rights-holders. International human rights frameworks, including the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child and its General Comment No. 25, stress that children‚Äôs rights apply online as much as offline.

Rights & Governance

Key Rights Relevant to Smart Toys

Right to privacy Right to information Right to protection from exploitation Right to play Right to be heard

Children consulted globally have said they want:

  • Clear explanations of what data is collected and why.
  • Control over what is saved, shared, or deleted.
  • Protection from excessive surveillance at home and school.
  • Safe opportunities to participate online without being profiled.

Why Rights-Based Design Matters

A rights-based approach shifts the responsibility away from children and parents alone and towards:

  • Companies that design and sell smart toys.
  • Policymakers who regulate data collection, storage, and sharing.
  • Educators and institutions that introduce digital systems to children.

Instead of expecting families to ‚Äúread the fine print,‚Äù a rights-respecting system would require companies to collect less data by default, minimise profiling, and provide child-friendly explanations of their practices.
